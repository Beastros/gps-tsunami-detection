How It Works

When a large underwater earthquake occurs, it displaces the seafloor and shoves the entire water column upward. That displacement sends a pressure wave not just across the ocean surface as a tsunami — it also propagates upward through the atmosphere all the way to the ionosphere, a layer of charged particles approximately 300 km above the Earth where GPS satellite signals pass through.

That pressure wave slightly disturbs the electron density in the ionosphere. GPS receivers on the ground are sensitive enough to measure it as a change in Total Electron Content (TEC) — the integrated number of electrons along the signal path. This phenomenon has been documented in the scientific literature. This pipeline attempts to detect it automatically, in real time, and fuse it with independent physical measurements to produce a calibrated confidence score.

The 7-Channel Sensor Stack

GPS / GLONASS / GALILEO TEC
RINEX observation files downloaded from NASA CDDIS for anchor stations across the Pacific. L1/L2 carrier phase differencing extracts TEC perturbations per satellite arc. Multi-constellation agreement adds confidence.
dTEC/dt — Wave-Front Rate
The time derivative of TEC. A tsunami-generated atmospheric gravity wave produces a characteristic onset rate distinct from background ionospheric drift. Corroboration adds +0.05 to combined confidence.
DART Ocean Pressure Buoys
28 NOAA NDBC deep-ocean pressure sensors across the Pacific. A 3-sigma anomaly in bottom pressure within the expected tsunami travel-time window adds up to +0.45. Negative DART reduces confidence.
GIRO Digisonde foF2
5 ionosonde stations monitored via the Global Ionosphere Radio Observatory DIDBase API. Independent foF2 perturbation confirmation adds +0.12.
Space Weather Quality Gate
4 NOAA SWPC feeds — Kp index, IMF Bz, solar wind speed, GOES X-ray flux. Geomagnetic storms produce ionospheric disturbances that mimic tsunami signals. Elevated space weather reduces TEC reliability weighting.
ShakeMap Focal Mechanism
USGS moment tensor API. Strike-slip earthquakes do not displace the water column and cannot generate tsunamis. Events below a tsunamigenic index of 0.25 are skipped entirely. Above that threshold the index scales the TEC contribution as a prior.

Why It Could Matter

Current operational tsunami warning relies primarily on DART buoys — physical sensors on the ocean floor that must wait for the wave to pass over them. The ionospheric signal travels through the atmosphere and may arrive at GPS stations before the wave reaches the nearest buoy. If that timing advantage holds on live events, even 20–30 minutes of additional warning time has real consequences for coastal communities.
